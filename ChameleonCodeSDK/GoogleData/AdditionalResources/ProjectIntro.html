Augmented Reality Treasure Hunt

ARTH Conceptual Map

Game storyboard:

  1. Download app, create character profile, read rules
  2. Character will have a “vice” and will require weekly recharging at local businesses (who have paid to be included). Pick a vice (it could be coffee, alcohol, or a bookstore or anything really). Recharging works just like a Pokestop.
  3. Game follows a storyline. Read first chapter, and receive first clue (some hints provided in story). Clue provides cryptic directions to a location. When the user deciphers the clue and reaches the location, they must prove that they didn’t just get there by accident, by opening app and choosing to use one of a limited number of verifications. The location will include a prominent feature, indicated by the clue, (like a word on the wall, or a slogan, or a street sign, and the prompt will provide the number of letters, and the first letter)
  4. Once verified, the user receives the next clue. That clue takes them to another destination, where they must answer timed trivia questions (fast enough to make it very hard to have time to google the correct answers on another device) to receive the next clue.
  5. At next clue, user must defeat a really annoying video game (like Flappy Bird or something) to receive the final clue.
  6. At the final clue, user must perform some kind of physical action verifiable by GPS (like walking around a statue 24 times)
  7. The first to complete all of the clues and arrive at the final destination will receive a message through the app declaring them the winner. All other app users will be notified that the first prize has been awarded. Users can keep playing for 2nd, 3rd (if there are prizes) or just for fun.

Technical aspects of app

App development: The app must be able to verify geographic location, and then deny or accept user’s verification request. Each verification will work like a password, not allowing access to the next part of the app without the correct answer. The annoying game will also require a developer.

Story development: Characters need compelling names, illustrations, and the chapters of the story should be entertaining.

Clue development: Consult historians, museums for trivia for clues and get cryptographers to help.

Risks:

  2. The clues getting leaked
  3. Websites that reveal answers to clues
